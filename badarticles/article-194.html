AUD Decline In Short Term Sets The Stage For Rally On Fundamentals In Medium Term

By Nick Nasad on September 12, 2011 | More Posts By Nick Nasad | Author's Website
  • Subscribe by RSS
  • Email/Share Page
  • Tweet

Summary: While the AUD is being battered by a “flight to safety”, and a move away from riskier commodity-based higher-yielding currencies, the Australian dollar still maintains its key fundamental advantages. Those are a high interest rate for the developed world, a strong mining boom fed by a “soft landing” in China, and a positive trade and current account which puts Australia away from the dangers of a credit downgrade.

Therefore the lower the AUD goes the cheaper it becomes for those that are bullish on its medium-term prospects. While in the 4H timeframe we do seem a bit oversold and therefore we may try to anticipate a short-term bounce the the upside, the uncertainty around the euro zone sovereign debt situation will likely pressure the AUD further in the short term.

If we drop back to the parity or even the lows we reached in early August (0.9925), that could present a buying opportunity. If equities stabilize even sooner – the middle of the week – then we may see the support earlier.

AUD/USD Recent Tumble Could Set The Stage for Rally on Fundamentals in Medium Term

The AUD/USD has been in a steep tumble as a result of risk aversion fears over the past 3 sessions.

To start this week we have slipped below the and have undone the rally 50% retracement of our upswing since August 9th, the 200-daily ema, as well as breaking below the pivot at 1.0310. If we close near the current prices, it would be a very bearish candle, and reminds us of the turmoil we saw in financial markets and in the AUD/USD from the beginning of August.

A further deterioration in the situation in Europe can continue this risk-off rout, with the RSI still above oversold levels. A target of the 61.8% retracement near 1.0250 and our pivot and 78.6% retracement at 1.0110 would be targets to the downside.

However, looking at the 4H chart, we seem much more oversold:

A break of 1.0275 would open up further downside risk as described above, but we could also see a correction to the recent two-session swing after the pair tagged the 30 level in the RSI.

If, and granted its a big IF considering the jitteriness of markets, but if we get some stabilization here, and equities can rebound, the AUD/USD is certainly off its most recent levels, and could be an an interesting buy.

The lower the AUD goes, the more its fundamental positives should kick in and help it to rebound, similar to what happened in mid-August. Following a proper bottoming, the pair should resume a move back above the 200-daily ema, and retest its highs from the beginning of the month (1.0750).

Interest Rate Yields Still Favor Aussie

One of the clear positives for the Australian dollar remains its interest-rate yield advantage against other developed nations – such as the US, Japan and Germany. As bond prices rise when yields fall, the fact that the yield has been sliding sharply over the last 6 months means traders were making money buying Australia’s bonds.  The Reserve Bank of Australia has scope to lower interest rates in case of its economy faltering is also an advantage to those trading Australian bonds. For those investors looking for better yields but still wanting a country with relatively safe credit risk, Australia fits the bill.

Here’s a look at the Australian 10-Year yield over the last year:

From Bloomberg: “Australian government bonds are poised for their biggest quarterly rally in more than two years as investors seeking alternatives to the U.S. dollar and euro buy the so-called Aussie. The nation’s debt has delivered a 5.33 percent return this quarter, the most since posting a 9.18 percent gain in the last three months of 2008, Bank of America Corp. indexes show.

Though Australian bond yields are quite low historically they’re still very high on an international comparable basis and that is what’s important for the currency,” said Richard Grace, the Sydney-based chief foreign-exchange strategist and head of international economics at Commonwealth Bank of Australia. “Sovereign downgrades in the U.S. and Europe and the very low interest-rate environment that’s likely to persist in both economies for some time means reserve managers are looking elsewhere for yield.”

Australia’s benchmark 10-year government bond yield fell 12 basis points, or 0.12 percentage point, to 4.15 percent, set for its lowest close since March 2009. While that is down from this year’s high of 5.84 percent in February, the rate compares with 1.92 percent on similar-maturity notes in the U.S., 1.77 percent in Germany and 0.99 percent in Japan.”

 

Trade Data Misses Forecasts, But Shows Another Solid Month of Surplus

Data on Sunday also show that Australia managed another month of trade surplus. Both exports and imports fell in Australia during July, keeping the trade balance steady at June’s downwardly revised levels near A$1.83 billion.

From AFP: “Australia in July posted its fifth monthly trade surplus in a row after a small deficit in February linked to floods and other weather-related disruptions, data showed on Monday.

The Australian Bureau of Statistics said the surplus widened slightly during the month, with the balance on goods and services at a seasonally-adjusted Aus$1.826 billion (US$1.894 billion). This was lower than analysts’ expectations of Aus$1.9 billion and compares with a downwardly-revised surplus of $1.817 billion in June.

During July, exports were down 1.0 percent in adjusted terms to Aus$26.1 billion with the month-on-month fall driven mainly by a 12 percent decline in coal shipments and a 19 percent fall in “other mineral fuels”. Manufactured exports rose six percent while metals — excluding gold — gained 18 percent.”

The data could be interpreted as a miss, considering coal exports fell 12% compared with June, meaning the recovery from storms at the beginning of the year is taking longer than expected. Most of the financial press reported it as a solid gain, but Australian stocks were ravaged by risk-off environment and the situation in Europe.

 

Labor Market Data Last Week for August Showed Some Weakness

While traded it was positive last week we saw weakness in the labor market. For the month of August they Australian economy shed 9.7K jobs, which was much lower than the 10.4K increase expected by economists. In July the economy also shed jobs to the tune of 4.1K. The unemployment rate meanwhile rose to 5.3% from 5.1%.

The labor market report should support the case of the RBA to stop its interest-rate increases, though so far the RBA has not showed a willingness to cut interest rates in order to help the economy – something that should help boost the AUD. Economic data has been a mixed batch over the last few months as mining sector profits continue to boost investment, while the other sectors in their economy – including manufacturing and services and construction – have been pressured.

 

Economy Still Strong as GDP, Company Profits, and Services PMI On Plus Side

Despite that, GDP for the second quarter rose 1.2% a bigger than expected reading on the 1% forecast.

Company operating profits were up 6.7% in the quarter compared to a 2.2% drop in the first quarter.

And, even the services PMI came in above the 50 level in August, a positive sign for that sector which had been printing readings at or below 50 for 3 months.

Final Thoughts: The fundamentals therefore continue to favor a strong Aussie once we are able to get through the risk aversion wrote about by the current bout of sovereign debt where is the euro zone. How far the fall will be in the AUD/USD is still to be determined but any bottoming action and move back in favor of the Australian dollar once equities stabilize should be supported by the country’s fundamentals.
